Decision Accountability Timeline

When reconstructability fails, defensibility fails.

When a decision is questioned, what matters is what can be proven. Most organisations assume they can show what was known at the time. They usually discover the gap only when they are asked to evidence it.

  1. Before a decision

    Will this be defensible later?

    “If this decision is questioned later, could I defend it?”

    • What will actually be preserved?
    • What will still be visible later?
    • Can the decision basis be shown exactly?

    Without certainty, confidence drops and decisions slow.

  2. After a decision

    What actually happened?

    “Can we show what actually happened?”

    • Teams start pulling logs and fragmented records
    • Gaps appear between assumptions and evidence
    • Different versions of events begin to emerge

    The issue shifts from the outcome to whether the explanation can be trusted.

  3. Under scrutiny

    Can we prove it?

    “Show exactly what was known at the time.”

    • Explanations are no longer enough
    • Missing evidence becomes visible to others
    • Accountability turns on what can be shown

    At this point, only what can be verified remains.

The real question

The real question is not whether a decision made sense at the time. It is whether you can still show exactly what was known when scrutiny arrives.

This is what changes.
